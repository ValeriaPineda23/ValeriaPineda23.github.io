Factors to improve online education, a study on the impact of COVID-19 on Delhi students

Due to the outbreak of COVID-19, people worldwide were asked to self-quarantine in their homes, resulting in the youth having to continue their education online. The lockdown and the effects of the pandemic had implications on students’ mental health, which presented as frustration, stress, and depression. The latter is not ideal for a healthy learning environment, as it leads to many coping mechanisms. This study analyzes a database that compiles the habits of 1182 individuals of different age groups from various educational institutes in Delhi-National Capital Region (NCR), India. This study identified factors that could improve the online education experience to facilitate the learning process while caring for students’ mental health worldwide. A CRISP-DM methodology was followed, and as a result, a model was built capable of predicting the score for an online class by analyzing the students’ daily habits.

Google Mobility Analysis of Mexico during COVID-19

Analyzed trends and seasonality behaviors of each mobility indicator from the Google Mobility Report of Mexico during COVID-19. A highly negative trend appeared for the first two months of lockdown in almost all indicators (except for Residential mobility). In addition, we encountered seasonality of 7, 14, and 21 days, which means that the weeks' pattern constantly repeats. Finally, we subtracted a time shift from the current time to avoid a non-stationary behavior. With the previous information, we developed an Auto-Regressive model that could predict Workplace mobility for November of 2020 with an RMSE of 6.32 for the training and 13.44 in the test set.

Effect of adding omitted variables in Mediation and Moderation Analyses

Mediation and moderation analyses have been widely implemented in social sciences. Both techniques use regression analysis for modeling. Consequently, these techniques are subjected to assumptions of linearity, normality, constant variance, and independence of errors. When these assumptions are violated, models generate distorted results on the magnitude of effects and the causal relationships. To remediate the appearing inconsistencies, a common practice is the inclusion of variables that exert specific effects given their nature. In this project, a case study is analyzed based on the work developed by Mukuka et al. (2021), where we explore the results of adding moderators. Here, we compare the robustness and assumptions fulfillment from the original model with our proposed model. Results show that the developed model increases the coefficient of determination by approximately 43.96%, and decreases Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) by 7.9 and 6.12 units, respectively.
